Solution to limit the carbon emission

Eat locally-produced and organic food

It has been estimated that 13% of U.S. greenhouse gas emissions result from the production and transport of food. Transporting food requires petroleum-based fuels, and many fertilizers are also fossil fuel-based.Eating only locally grown food for one year would save the greenhouse gas equivalent of driving 1,000 miles, but eating just one vegetarian meal a week for a year saves 160 miles more than that, according to one study from researchers at Carnegie Mellon.

Boosting efficiency of livestock production and resource use

Technological innovations such as improved feeding, genetics, animal health, general husbandry and information technology are driving up productivity, making resource use more efficient with potential to reduce environmental impact.Emission intensities vary widely within and across livestock systems – particularly for ruminants – showing that better management practices can be adopted more widely to boost production efficiency.

Intensifying recycling efforts and minimizing losses for a circular bioeconomy

Promoting a circular bioeconomy involves recycling resources at every possible step in agrifood systems and closing systems to minimize the loss of resources and nutrients.Manure and slaughterhouse waste can be used to generate fertilizer and biogas as a source of renewable energy.

Striving for healthy, sustainable diets and accounting for protein alternatives

The prevalence of malnutrition and overconsumption is unacceptably high with livestock protein consumption tied to wealth.New alternative proteins coming to market could potentially present consumers with climate smart choices with less environmental impact.
